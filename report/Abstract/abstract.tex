\begin{abstract}
Breast-cancer screening relies on mammography, yet the public datasets available for building computer-aided diagnosis systems are small, so convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on them tend to overfit; handcrafted, training-free texture features such as the wavelet scattering transform (WST) offer a stable complement. This dissertation presents a Python replication of the CNN--wavelet-scattering fusion framework of Razali et al.\ on the INbreast dataset, evaluated on two distinct tasks: fatty-versus-fibroglandular tissue-density classification and benign-versus-malignant mass classification. The pipeline comprises percentile-clip normalisation, ground-truth pectoral-muscle removal and CLAHE preprocessing; strictly image-level (group-aware) data splitting to prevent patch-level leakage; wavelet-scattering and fine-tuned ResNet18 feature branches; and their feature-level fusion. Beyond replication, the wavelet-scattering branch is extended along two threads: a scattering-covariance descriptor for tissue, and --- for mass --- a representation that preserves the scattering spatial map at a lower invariance scale and learns over it with a small CNN head, with a lesion-relative region-aware decomposition as its interpretable instantiation and a late-fusion diagnostic. All headline results use a repeated five-seed, AUROC-led protocol. The tissue CNN and fusion models are near-saturated, and scattering covariance is the strongest tissue descriptor (AUROC ${\approx}0.99$); on mass, paper-style fusion improves on either branch alone, scattering covariance gives no gain, while preserving the scattering map and learning over it lifts mass AUROC from ${\approx}0.71$ to ${\approx}0.92$ (whole-patch map-CNN ${\approx}0.92$; the region-aware instantiation ${\approx}0.91$, on par within seed noise), and a decision-level late fusion is numerically strongest (${\approx}0.94$) but is reported as an ensemble rather than the core contribution. The central finding is that, for tissue texture, richer \emph{scalar} scattering descriptors suffice, whereas for mass lesions \emph{preserving the spatial structure} of the representation matters more than changing scalar features or classifiers --- though, on the small mass set (${\sim}24$ held-out patches), all mass estimates carry wide across-seed bands.
\end{abstract}
