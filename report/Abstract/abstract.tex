\begin{abstract}
Breast-cancer screening relies on mammography, but the public datasets used to build computer-aided diagnosis systems are small, so convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on them tend to overfit; handcrafted, training-free texture features such as the wavelet scattering transform (WST) offer a stable complement. This dissertation reproduces, in Python, the CNN--WST feature-fusion framework of Razali et al.\ on the INbreast dataset, across two tasks: fatty-versus-fibroglandular tissue-density classification and benign-versus-malignant mass classification. Under a leakage-free, image-level evaluation protocol, the replication reproduces the paper's three configurations (WS-only, CNN-only, and their fusion) on both tasks, though the paper's headline mass AU-ROC of $1.00$ does not survive repeated evaluation.

Beyond replication, the wavelet-scattering branch is extended along two threads: a richer scalar scattering-covariance descriptor for tissue, and, for mass, a representation that preserves the scattering spatial map and learns over it with a small CNN, instantiated as an interpretable lesion-relative region decomposition. Scattering covariance is the strongest tissue descriptor (AU-ROC ${\approx}0.98$) but gives no gain on mass; there, preserving the spatial map instead lifts AU-ROC from ${\approx}0.73$ to ${\approx}0.95$. A final preprocessing-sensitivity step then shows that removing the CLAHE contrast-enhancement stage (extracting the lesion patches from masked, percentile-normalised images) improves the region-aware model further, from ${\approx}0.95$ to AU-ROC ${\approx}0.97$ with all four regions, and up to ${\approx}0.99$ for the best region configuration. The central finding is that tissue texture is well served by a richer scalar descriptor, whereas mass lesions require preserving the spatial structure of the representation rather than changing scalar features or classifiers. On the small mass set (${\sim}24$ held-out patches per split), all mass estimates carry wide across-seed bands.
\end{abstract}
