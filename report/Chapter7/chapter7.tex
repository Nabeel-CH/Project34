\chapter{Conclusion} \label{sec:conclusion}

This dissertation reproduced the CNN, wavelet-scattering (WS), and CNN~+~WS fusion branches of Razali et al.\ \cite{razali2023cnn} (their Models~1, 2 and~5) on the INbreast dataset, and re-assessed their reported performance under a stricter, repeated evaluation protocol. On the tissue-density task the reproduction is faithful: the CNN (AU-ROC $0.979$) and CNN~+~WS fusion ($0.988$) are near-saturated and exceed the paper (which we attribute to a cleaner preprocessing pipeline rather than a methodological advance) while averaged WS trails ($0.895$ against the paper's $0.94$), the largest replication gap. On the mass task the assessment is more critical: the paper's headline fusion AU-ROC of $1.00$ rests on a single ${\sim}22$-patch test split and does not survive the leakage-free, repeated protocol adopted here, itself a methodological contribution, settling at $0.816$ under five seeds. The qualitative claim it rests on, that handcrafted scattering features compensate for a data-starved CNN, does nonetheless hold.

Beyond replication, two extensions to the wavelet-scattering branch form the project's contribution and, together, its principal finding. For the tissue task a richer scalar descriptor suffices: the scattering covariance of Cheng and M\'enard ($J=6$, $L=3$) reaches AU-ROC $0.984$, the strongest tissue WS representation in the project. For the mass task, scalar descriptors do not help (scattering covariance gives $0.734$, no gain over averaged WS) and what matters instead is preserving the spatial structure of the scattering representation. Retaining the scattering maps at a lower invariance scale and learning over them with a small CNN lifts mass AU-ROC from $0.726$ to $0.909$; conditioning those maps on lesion morphology, in a lesion-relative region-aware decomposition, reaches $0.946$, rising to ${\approx}0.97$--$0.99$ once CLAHE is removed. Across both tasks the lesson is consistent: tissue rewards a richer scalar descriptor, whereas mass rewards preserving the spatial structure of the representation.

The INbreast mass set is small ($115$ patches, ${\sim}24$ held out per split), so every mass estimate carries a wide across-seed band, and the strongest region-aware configurations overlap within that noise; all are reported as five-seed means~$\pm$~standard deviation under leakage-free, image-level splitting. Only a single dataset is evaluated and, following the paper, no data augmentation is used.

Several directions follow. The most valuable is external validation on additional mammography datasets (for example CBIS-DDSM or MIAS, available through The Cancer Imaging Archive) to test whether the region-aware mass improvement generalises beyond INbreast. Reproducing the CNN~+~WS fusion on the scattering-covariance features rather than the standard coefficients, and replacing CLAHE with the paper's density-aware contrast enhancement to close the residual WS-tissue gap, would each sharpen the reproduction. Learned regularisation priors and alternative architectures are a further avenue; these, with light data augmentation (avoided here to follow the paper), could curb the small-sample CNN overfitting that recurs throughout this work and strengthen interpretable biomarker selection. Together these would test whether preserving the spatial scattering map is the decisive lever for mass classification that this work suggests it is.
