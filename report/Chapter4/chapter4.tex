\chapter{Paper-Style Replication Results} \label{sec:results}

This chapter reports the paper-style replication for both tasks, method by method. Numbers quoted in the text are for the seed-34 image-level split; the two consolidated tables (Tables~\ref{tab:results_tissue} and~\ref{tab:results_mass}) give the headline five-seed protocol (seeds $\{1,7,34,42,99\}$; Section~\ref{sec:eval_protocol}) as mean~$\pm$~standard deviation. The wavelet-scattering (WS) branch is deterministic, so its seed-34 figure equals the five-seed protocol's seed-34 fold; for the trained CNN and fusion branches we quote that same fold, so every value below is the corresponding row of the final tables. The paper labels its configurations Model~1 (CNN-only), Model~2 (WS-only) and Model~5 (CNN~+~WS fusion)---the three configurations of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Its remaining Models~3, 4 and~6 add a GLCM texture branch, left out of scope here because the paper's own results show GLCM adds no accuracy, precision, recall or F1 and only $+0.01$ AU-ROC over CNN~+~WS---it cannot change any conclusion drawn in this project.

\textbf{Outcomes at a glance.} The tissue task reproduces and mildly exceeds the paper: averaged WS reaches AU-ROC $0.895$, and the CNN ($0.979$) and CNN~+~WS fusion ($0.988$) are near-saturated. The mass task is harder and more fragile: averaged WS is weak (AU-ROC $0.726$), fusion is the strongest paper-style model ($0.816$), and the paper's headline mass AU-ROC of $1.00$ does not survive a repeated, leakage-free protocol.

\textbf{Differences from the paper.} Four implementation choices distinguish our pipeline from Razali et al.\ and account for the discrepancies discussed below: standard CLAHE rather than their density-aware histogram adjustment; the Kymatio scattering library (406 coefficients) rather than their MATLAB toolbox (391); strict image-level splitting (Section~\ref{sec:stage3}) rather than their ambiguous ``eight-to-two'' split; and a reproducible \texttt{Name="Mass"} XML extraction giving 116 patches (115 after the data-quality filter) rather than their 112. Each is revisited where it bears on a result.

\section{Evaluation: image-level splitting} \label{sec:stage3}

Every train/test split and cross-validation fold in this chapter is grouped by source mammogram: all patches cropped from one image are kept on the same side of the split (\texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} with \texttt{groups=file\_id}; Section~\ref{sec:eval_protocol}). This image-level protocol is essential because patches from the same mammogram are strongly correlated (same patient, same view, often overlapping tissue), so a patch-level split, which scatters correlated patches across the partition, lets a model recognise test patches from their training siblings rather than generalise to unseen patients. We saw this directly: an initial patch-level split returned an implausible $100\%$ on every metric, and auditing it found 59 of the 100 source images on both sides of the partition, with 86 of the 87 test patches ($98.9\%$) sharing a source mammogram with the training set: near-total leakage. Switching to image-level splitting (grouping by \texttt{file\_id}) removed it, and is standard practice for multi-patch medical imaging. The paper's split description (\emph{``a randomized ratio of eight-to-two''}) is ambiguous on this point, and its CNN-only cross-validation/test gap ($0.99$ vs $0.774$) is more consistent with the stricter image-level reading, which we adopt throughout.

\section{Wavelet scattering baseline (fatty vs fibroglandular)} \label{sec:stage1}

The paper's Model~2 (WS-only) applies the Kymatio scattering transform (Section~\ref{sec:ws}) to each of the 434 tissue patches, spatially averages the coefficients to a 406-dimensional vector, and classifies them with the Subspace k-NN ensemble. Seed-34 group-aware cross-validation accuracy was $81.2\% \pm 7.3\%$ and the held-out AU-ROC $0.926$ (test accuracy $0.844$; full metrics in Table~\ref{tab:results_tissue}). This is qualitatively consistent with the paper ($\sim$91\%) but trails by $\sim$7~pp, most plausibly because of the contrast-enhancement and scattering-library differences noted above (Table~\ref{tab:ws_config}). The WS branch is revisited with a richer representation (the scattering-covariance extension) in Section~\ref{sec:scov_tissue}.

\section{CNN-only classification (tissue)} \label{sec:stage4}

The CNN branch (Section~\ref{sec:cnn}) fine-tunes ResNet18 and is evaluated two ways: directly via the softmax head, and via 512-dimensional average-pool features classified by the same Subspace k-NN ensemble (the paper's Model~1 protocol). The softmax head reached $97.8\%$ test accuracy with a modest overfit gap of $2.2\%$; under the paper protocol, cross-validation was saturated ($100.0\% \pm 0.0\%$) and held-out test accuracy was $98.9\%$ at AU-ROC $0.987$ (Table~\ref{tab:results_tissue}). The CNN substantially exceeds the paper's $77.4\%$, and its overfit gap ($\sim$2\%) is far milder than the $21.6\%$ cross-validation-versus-test gap the paper reports. The likeliest cause is the cleaner preprocessing pipeline (percentile-clipped normalisation, ground-truth pectoral masks, CLAHE, and ACR-guided patch selection), which is consistent with the WS branch trailing by $\sim$7~pp on the same, identically preprocessed patches.

\section{Mass classification} \label{sec:stage5}

The benign-versus-malignant task (Section~\ref{sec:mass_rois}) applies both branches unchanged. The 116 mass patches built from the XML boxes (75 malignant, 41 benign; the paper has $76+36$) reduce to the 115-patch set the tables use once the data-quality filter drops one all-zero patch (Section~\ref{sec:mass_quality_filter}); the seed-34 split holds out 25 patches.

\textbf{WS-only.} Seed-34 cross-validation accuracy was $71.1\% \pm 11.3\%$ and held-out AU-ROC $0.765$ (test accuracy $0.68$). Because the held-out set is $\sim$25 patches, a single split is uninformative (across the five seeds held-out AU-ROC ranges $0.70$--$0.76$ and accuracy $0.64$--$0.83$), so the five-seed mean is the figure we report: AU-ROC $0.726 \pm 0.030$, accuracy $0.701 \pm 0.075$ (Table~\ref{tab:results_mass}). The cross-validation accuracy is close to, and slightly above, the paper's $69\%$. Averaged WS is clearly a weaker descriptor for masses than for background tissue, consistent with the paper's observation that mass patches yield fewer discriminative scattering features.

\textbf{CNN-only.} A fresh ResNet18 (not warm-started from the tissue model) overfits sharply on the small sample: train $100\%$, validation $66.7\%$, test $84.0\%$ (overfit gap $16.0\%$). Under the paper protocol, cross-validation was $98.9\%$ (optimistic, see below) and held-out test accuracy $76.0\%$ at AU-ROC $0.750$ (Table~\ref{tab:results_mass}). The per-epoch curves show the paper's overfitting signature (Figure~\ref{fig:mass_overfit}): training accuracy saturates by epoch~10 while validation accuracy oscillates and validation loss climbs.

\section{CNN~+~WS feature fusion} \label{sec:stage6}

The paper's headline model (Model~5) concatenates the CNN and WS vectors into the $918$-dimensional fusion input of Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}, classified by the same Subspace k-NN ensemble---no feature selection or dimensionality reduction---with the scaler fitted on the training fold only. Fusion applies to both tasks, so tissue and mass results appear together here.

\textbf{Tissue.} Fusion is near-saturated (cross-validation $100\%$; test $97.8\%$, AU-ROC $0.987$; Table~\ref{tab:results_tissue}) but does not improve on the already-saturated CNN-only in accuracy (seed-34 $97.8\%$ versus $98.9\%$; five-seed $0.964$ versus $0.979$); its only consistent edge is a marginally higher five-seed AU-ROC ($0.988$ versus $0.979$). Both exceed the paper's $93.5\%$, for the same reason as the CNN-only result.

\textbf{Mass.} At seed-34, fusion and CNN-only tie on accuracy (both $0.76$). The fusion advantage instead appears in the five-seed means: AU-ROC $0.816 \pm 0.057$ against the CNN's $0.762$ and WS's $0.726$, and accuracy $0.792$ against $0.766$ (Table~\ref{tab:results_mass}). This is the regime the paper emphasises: handcrafted scattering features add information exactly when the CNN is data-starved and overfitting. The paper's mass AU-ROC of $1.00$ rests on a single $\sim$22-patch split; our repeated estimate ($0.816$) is the stable figure.

\textbf{On the optimistic CV.} The strong cross-validation figures on learned features ($100\%$, $98.9\%$, $97.8\%$) are optimistic and marked ``(opt.)'' in the tables: because the CNN features were extracted once, \emph{before} the CV folds were drawn, the feature extractor has effectively seen every fold's test patches, so CV overstates out-of-sample accuracy. The held-out AU-ROC is therefore the metric we trust.

\section{Why the WS branch needs extending} \label{sec:why_extension}

Two facts motivate the extensions of Chapter~\ref{sec:extensions}. First, the \emph{tissue} task is essentially solved: the CNN (AU-ROC $0.979$) and fusion ($0.988$) are near-saturated and averaged WS is already respectable ($0.895$), leaving little headroom. Second, the mass task is harder and more fragile: the held-out set is tiny, the across-seed spread is wide (Figure~\ref{fig:mass_perseed}), and the paper-style \emph{averaged} WS is weak (AU-ROC $0.726$, below the CNN's $0.762$ and fusion's $0.816$). Fusion's $0.816$ is the honest ceiling of the paper-style replication on mass (far short of the paper's seed-lucky $1.00$), so any real mass gain must come from changing the representation, not the classifier.

Averaging is the prime suspect: collapsing each scattering map to a scalar discards \emph{where} lesion structure sits, the core-versus-margin-versus-context signal that separates benign from malignant masses (Chapter~\ref{sec:background}). The next chapter asks whether the WS branch improves with (a)~a richer scalar descriptor or (b)~a representation that preserves the spatial map. It screens the non-region-aware options (normalisation, statistical and pooled WS, scattering covariance, CNN crops, fusion variants), finds their gains useful but limited, and then commits, for the mass task, to preserving the scattering spatial map and learning over it with a small CNN, with a lesion-relative region-aware decomposition as its interpretable instantiation, lifting the mass AU-ROC from $0.726$ to ${\sim}0.95$ (Chapter~\ref{sec:extensions}).
