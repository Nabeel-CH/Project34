\chapter{Extension Experiments} \label{sec:extensions}

The extension work is organised around a single question raised by the replication: the paper-style averaged wavelet scattering is a weak mass descriptor (AU-ROC $0.726$, barely above the CNN's $0.762$ and below fusion's $0.816$), so what actually rescues it? The answer, and the chapter's main methodological contribution, is that \textbf{preserving the wavelet-scattering spatial map and learning over it with a small CNN} is the decisive lever, instantiated as a lesion-relative \emph{region-aware} decomposition (Section~\ref{sec:region_aware}). We reach it by first screening many scalar-feature and classifier variants, none of which move the mass task (Section~\ref{sec:screening}); we then develop the region-aware model and its comparators in depth; and we show that a lighter, no-CLAHE input strengthens it further (Section~\ref{sec:region_noclahe}). A secondary, tissue-side result, that a richer scalar scattering-covariance descriptor suffices where the mass task instead needs spatial structure, is reported briefly in Section~\ref{sec:scov_tissue}. Unless stated otherwise, all numbers use the locked five-seed image-level protocol of Section~\ref{sec:eval_protocol}.

\section{Screening: many methods, one lever} \label{sec:screening}

Before committing to a deep extension, a broad set of non-region-aware methods was screened under the locked protocol (Table~\ref{tab:screening_overview}), with mass AU-ROC as the headline metric. The pattern is consistent: changing the scalar feature (WS normalisation, statistical/per-path WS, pooled-grid summaries, scattering covariance) or the classifier moves mass performance only within across-seed noise of the averaged-WS baseline ($0.726$); frozen-CNN crops and whole-patch fusions top out around $0.86$--$0.91$. The one method that preserves the spatial map and learns over it, a small CNN on the full-patch $J=3$ scattering maps ($0.909$), jumps clear of every scalar variant. Preserving spatial structure, not enriching a scalar descriptor, is therefore the decisive factor, and the rest of the chapter pursues it (Section~\ref{sec:region_aware}). On the tissue task the opposite holds: several structured scalar summaries already saturate it, and the scattering-covariance descriptor is the adopted tissue-side extension (Section~\ref{sec:scov_tissue}).

\section{Region-aware wavelet scattering (mass)} \label{sec:region_aware}

Averaged WS fails on mass because it discards \emph{where} lesion structure lies; rather than enrich a scalar descriptor, the region-aware model keeps the scattering maps at a lower invariance scale and lets a small CNN learn over them, with a lesion-relative decomposition as its interpretable, morphology-conditioned instantiation.

\subsection*{Construction}
The extension has four ingredients. \textbf{Region masks.} Each \texttt{Name="Mass"} polygon is rasterised, and four lesion-relative regions are derived by morphology (a full region (lesion plus margin), an inner eroded core, a boundary annulus, and a context surround), sized as a fraction of the lesion box so the decomposition scales with lesion size. \textbf{$J=3$ scattering maps.} Each region is scattered at $J=3, L=5$ rather than the paper's $J=6$, retaining a $28\times28$ grid of $91$ coefficient channels per region ($28\times28$ from downsampling the $224$-pixel patch by $2^{J}{=}8$; $91 = 1$ order-0 $+\,15$ order-1 $+\,75$ order-2 paths) instead of the near-scalar grid $J=6$ produces; stacking the four regions gives a $4\times91$-channel, $28\times28$ tensor that preserves where energy sits. \textbf{WS-map CNN head.} A small network (two $3\times3$ Conv--BN--ReLU blocks ($32$ channels), global average pooling, dropout $0.5$, linear classifier; Adam ($\eta=10^{-3}$, weight decay $3\times10^{-4}$), $60$ epochs, batch $24$) learns the spatial relationships a hand-pooled summary discards. \textbf{Comparators.} A classical region-aware baseline (per-channel pooled statistics $\to$ logistic regression / Subspace k-NN, isolating the decomposition from the learned head), an improved CNN ($512$-d frozen-ResNet18 features on a square lesion crop $\to$ logistic regression), and a decision-level late fusion of the region-aware WS$\rightarrow$CNN and improved-CNN probabilities.

The spectrogram diagnostic of Figure~\ref{fig:region_ws_spectrogram} gives a visualisation-only view for a size-matched malignant/benign pair: pooling the first-order maps by scale and orientation on a shared colour scale, the malignant lesion's boundary region carries the strongest coarse-scale energy (consistent with the irregular, spiculated margins of malignancy) while the benign lesion's energies are lower and more even. Because the heatmap is itself a spatial average it over-states the boundary's role: the ablation below shows the learned CNN, which sees the full maps, does not lean on any single region ($0.946 \rightarrow 0.939$ when the boundary is dropped).

\subsection*{Results}
Table~\ref{tab:region_story} walks one change at a time from the paper-style baseline to the region-aware models; Figure~\ref{fig:region_story} plots the same progression. It is monotone and interpretable: moving from $J=6$ averaged WS to $J=3$ maps with a linear head already lifts AU-ROC from $0.726$ to $0.801$ (spatial detail helps even on the whole patch); conditioning on lesion regions raises the classical model to $0.892$; and letting a small CNN learn over the region maps reaches $\mathbf{0.946 \pm 0.036}$, the strongest \emph{standalone} mass model in the project. A whole-patch $J=3$-map CNN with no region decomposition already reaches $0.909$, so the decisive step is preserving the map and learning over it; the region decomposition adds a further, morphology-aware lift.

\textbf{Comparators (brief).} Two CNN-side comparators complement the region-aware model without beating it. A frozen ResNet18 with a logistic-regression head is far steadier than the fine-tuned CNN of the replication; varying only the crop gives tight $0.906$, square $0.857$, and expanded $0.820$ (tight and square overlap within noise; the square crop is the ``improved CNN'' comparator above). On the whole patch, feature fusion of the frozen CNN with averaged WS reaches $0.882$ and late-probability fusion $0.858$: neither approaches the region-aware branch. Late fusion helps (Table~\ref{tab:region_story}, $0.948$) precisely because one branch already preserves lesion structure: fusion amplifies a good spatial branch but cannot manufacture one from averaged features.

\textbf{Stability and headline.} Because the WS-map CNN is learned on only ${\sim}90$ patches, its five-seed held-out AU-ROC carries split sensitivity ($\pm0.036$); a group-aware $10$-fold diagnostic gives a tighter $0.919 \pm 0.012$, confirming the held-out mean is not a lucky split. We report the held-out five-seed mean as the headline with the group-CV as the stability check. Late fusion is numerically strongest ($0.948$) but is a two-model ensemble whose margin over the region-aware WS$\rightarrow$CNN alone ($0.946$) is within noise, so the methodological contribution is the region-aware WS$\rightarrow$CNN itself: it turns the weak averaged-WS branch ($0.726$) into a strong, interpretable mass descriptor ($0.946$).

\section{Strengthening the region-aware model: masked, no-CLAHE inputs} \label{sec:region_noclahe}

A final axis of the mass extension is input sensitivity. Wavelet scattering is texture-sensitive, so CLAHE, which sharpens local contrast for viewing, may distort the coefficient energy scattering relies on. A natural design is therefore to let preprocessing mainly locate coordinates and regions, and extract the classification patches from less-altered images. This remains a sensitivity analysis, not a replacement for the paper-style replication.

\subsection*{Pixel-source screen}
Reusing the sampled patch coordinates and labels but changing the pixel source, four sources are compared: the current fully preprocessed images; original DICOM pixels with min--max scaling (\texttt{raw\_minmax}); with 1st/99th percentile scaling only (\texttt{raw\_percentile}); and \texttt{masked\_no\_clahe} (percentile normalisation, breast/background masking, and pectoral removal, but no CLAHE). The useful effect is concentrated in mass WS-only: tissue WS did \emph{not} improve (the current full-preprocessing row stays closest to the Razali reference, AU-ROC $0.895$ vs the paper's $0.940$; lighter variants fall to $0.881$/$0.812$), and CNN-only and fusion lowered under lighter inputs on both tasks (on mass, CNN-only $0.762\to0.644$, fusion $0.816\to0.722$). This is why the investigation narrows to mass WS and the region-aware WS-map methods. Figure~\ref{fig:preproc_levels} shows the WS-only comparison and Figure~\ref{fig:preproc_paper_full_original} all six task/method combinations.

\section{Scattering covariance for the tissue task} \label{sec:scov_tissue}

The tissue task is the mirror image of the mass task, and the extension there is correspondingly light. Tissue is near-saturated by the paper-style pipeline, and the only WS-branch gap to close is a scalar one. The scattering covariance of Cheng \& M\'enard~\cite{cheng2021scattering} augments the standard $S_0, S_1, S_2$ coefficients with cross-path correlation statistics; its construction, which avoids flattening the scattering tensor by summarising it into rotation-averaged scalars, is given in~\cite{cheng2021scattering}. Applied to the tissue patches under the same split, scaler, and classifier as the averaged-WS baseline, and tuned by group-aware CV on the training folds only over a small $J\times L$ grid (which selects $J=6, L=3$, $580$ features), it lifts tissue WS from AU-ROC $0.895$ to $\mathbf{0.984}$ and test accuracy from $0.839$ to $0.959$ (Table~\ref{tab:scov_tissue_initial}); an independent ``direct'' Cheng usage ($L=4$) gives $0.982$, confirming the result is not specific to one orientation count. This is the adopted tissue-side WS extension.

The same descriptor gives \emph{no} gain on mass: at its best setting it reaches AU-ROC $0.734 \pm 0.062$, indistinguishable from the $0.726$ averaged-WS baseline, and lowers F1 ($0.732$ vs $0.781$). Richer scalar statistics help the texture task but not the mass task. We adopt scattering covariance on the tissue side only; fusing it with the CNN, and a full mass $J\times L$ grid, are left as future work.

Two further WS variants, noted as negative controls\label{sec:ws_alternatives}: flattening the scattering map into one long vector (with and without PCA) and tensor-distance nearest-neighbour classifiers both failed to beat the simple spatial mean, reconfirming that the productive direction is lower-$J$ spatial maps fed to a learned head (Section~\ref{sec:region_aware}), not flattening or bespoke distances.
